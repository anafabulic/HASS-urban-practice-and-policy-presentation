How can urban mobility policy change?


a presentation for HASS02.542 Urban Practice and Policy II
Sean Chua / 1006466

tl;dr

Experts usually cite Singapore’s unique spatial constraints and political situation as reasons for Singapore’s transport policy success.

(these are usually treated as historical and geographical ‘givens’)

↓

but...

...the so-called unique political landscape includes lots of internal and external frictions.

↓

and so...

it’s more accurate to credit Singapore’s transport policy successes to the PAP invoking these ‘given facts’ in order to produce new, streamlined consensuses in Parliament.

what I did:

Trawled through 55 years of Parliament transcripts and NewspaperSG archives for mentions of ‘mobility’, ‘public transport’, ‘congestion’, etc.

(basically looking @ mobility through terrestrial, motorised transport—that’s the kind most-discussed in Parliament when it comes to spending and building things)

history

1955: Fear of gridlock drives planning urgency.

mid–1960s: Transport planning takes a backseat during drama of merger and separation with Malaysia.

1970: Minister of Communication Yong Nyuk Lin sets transport White Paper characterised by firm-minded rationalisation of bus services.

this sets a precedent for firm measures as
‘the only rational alternative’ in a congested city.

3 main threads

↓

1:

MRT too expensive—debated between Goh Keng Swee and Ong Teng Cheong in Parliament circa 1981.

Between the late ‘60s and early ‘70s, various backbenchers also raised issues of cost of appraisals vs. perceived slowness of construction.

Consensus gradually forged through technological necessity and futuristic/world-city imaginaries.

2:

Curbs on car ownership and use met with dissent and requests for clarifications starting in the mid–‘70s.

Mid–‘90s dissent raised by opposition SDP crushed by the PAP’s superior facts and figures.

The Electronic Road Pricing programme rolls out practically uncontested.

3:

The theme of climate change is tepidly raised in Parliament from the early ‘00s, only really gaining steam in 2022 post–COP26.

In the meantime, transport policy shies away from punitive measures and becomes more aspirational rather than necessary.

Consensus is tenuous as sustainability is not a unilateral affair.

so we see:

The PAP justified sweeping transport reform and punitive measures with the spectre of congestion and the necessity of high-technology.

Dissent was quashed and consensus was forged by invoking the need for technocratic measures in desperate times.

Beginning with the MRT, consensus was forged by invoking aspirational imaginaries.

We don’t see such a cohesive push towards green mobility... yet?

my (cynical, unfounded) take:

Need about 20 years to get over organisational inertia (‘60s–‘80s for MRT, ‘70s–‘90s for ERP).

We’ll get there by setting goals between 2030–2050.

But is it too late?

conclusion:

What is certain is that the old ways of generating consensus (desperation, technocratic rationality, global-city excellence) will rear their heads in new forms.

(but softening stances towards unilateral action and punitive measures suggest that different outcomes will follow.)

conclusion:

don’t credit policy success to predestined givens—look at where dissent happened & see how it was paved over
